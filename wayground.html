Surveillance

Read the text carefully. Then answer the three questions. Your teacher will discuss the answers with the class.

Surveillance
Michel Foucault (1926-1984)
French philosopher

Surveillance means watching or being watched. In everyday life, we think of surveillance as cameras or police. However, the French philosopher Michel Foucault argued that surveillance is much more powerful than this.

Foucault described a type of prison called the Panopticon. In this prison, one guard can see all prisoners, but the prisoners cannot see the guard. Because of this, prisoners start to control their own behaviour. They act as if they are always being watched, even when they are not (Foucault, 1977).

Foucault argued that modern society works in the same way. Schools, hospitals, and workplaces use systems of observation to control people. People change how they act, how they dress, and how they speak because they believe someone might be watching them.

When we look at art through this lens, we ask: who has the power to look? Who must be seen? Does being watched change how the person in the artwork presents their identity?

Reference (APA 7th)

Foucault, M. (1977). Discipline and punish: The birth of the prison (A. Sheridan, Trans.). Pantheon Books. (Original work published 1975)

Gender Norms
Judith Butler (born 1956)
American philosopher

Gender refers to the social ideas about how men and women should look, speak, and behave. These ideas change across different cultures and historical periods. The philosopher Judith Butler argues that gender is not natural or fixed. Instead, gender is a performance.

Butler explains that from the moment we are born, we are taught to perform gender. We learn what clothes to wear, how to walk, and how to speak based on whether we are seen as male or female (Butler, 1990). These repeated actions create the impression that gender is natural, but Butler argues this impression is not real.

Importantly, Butler says that people can also resist or challenge gender norms through their performance. By deliberately wearing unexpected clothes, taking unexpected poses, or presenting their body in unusual ways, people can make visible what is normally invisible: the fact that gender is constructed, not natural.

Artists frequently use gender as a subject. They show figures performing gender in expected ways, or they deliberately break these expectations to make the viewer think about rules they normally do not notice.

Reference (APA 7th)

Butler, J. (1990). Gender trouble: Feminism and the subversion of identity. Routledge.

Social Class

Social Class
Pierre Bourdieu (1930-2002)
French sociologist

Social class refers to the different groups in society based on wealth, education, and social position. The French sociologist Pierre Bourdieu developed an important theory about how class works in culture.

Bourdieu argued that class is not only about money. It also involves what he called "cultural capital." Cultural capital means the knowledge, tastes, and behaviours that are valued in a particular society (Bourdieu, 1984). For example, knowing which music is considered "serious" or which appearance is considered "professional" are all forms of cultural capital.

Bourdieu showed that cultural capital is not distributed equally. People from higher social classes learn this knowledge at home and in education. This gives them advantages in work and social life. People from lower social classes often do not have the same cultural knowledge, which limits their opportunities.

Art is deeply connected to class because art itself is a form of cultural capital. Who creates art, who appears in art, and who can visit galleries are all shaped by class. Artworks can show us who has power in a society and who does not.

Reference (APA 7th)

Bourdieu, P. (1984). Distinction: A social critique of the judgement of taste (R. Nice, Trans.). Harvard University Press. (Original work published 1979)
